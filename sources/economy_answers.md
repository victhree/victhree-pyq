# National Income & Macroeconomics

## GDP, GNP & National Income

**Q.106 (CDS 2016-II)** — Which statement is correct about the composition of national income in India?
a) The share of the manufacturing sector has declined.  b) The share of the services sector has increased sharply.  c) The share of agriculture has remained static.  d) The share of the services sector has declined.
**Correct Answer:** b) The share of services sector has increased sharply
**Explanation:** National income (GDP) is split among three sectors — primary (agriculture), secondary (manufacturing/industry) and tertiary (services). Most economies pass through a structural transformation: as they develop, the share of agriculture shrinks and the share of industry and then services rises. India is unusual in that it **leapt from an agrarian to a services-led economy**, partly skipping a large manufacturing phase — services (IT, finance, telecom, trade, transport) now contribute well over half of GDP, and their share has **risen sharply** since the 1991 reforms (b). Agriculture's share has fallen steadily to under a fifth (so it is not "static," ruling out c), manufacturing's share has stagnated rather than collapsed (a is too strong), and services have clearly grown, not declined (d is wrong).

**Q.72 (CDS 2017-I)** — About the Trans-Pacific Partnership (TPP): 1. Signed by 12 Pacific Rim nations in 2015. 2. Member countries account for about 40% of global GDP. 3. India is a founder member of TPP.
a) 1, 2 and 3  b) 1 and 2 only  c) 2 and 3 only  d) 1 only
**Correct Answer:** b) 1 and 2 only
**Explanation:** The **Trans-Pacific Partnership** was a mega-regional free-trade agreement among 12 Pacific-Rim economies (the US, Japan, Australia, Canada, Mexico, Vietnam, etc.), negotiations for which concluded in 2015 and which was signed in early 2016 — together these members made up roughly **40% of world GDP**, which is why it was billed as a potential "game-changer" in global trade (statements 1 and 2). **India was never part of the TPP** — it is not a Pacific-Rim trading bloc member — so statement 3 is false. (The US later withdrew in 2017, and the pact was reborn as the CPTPP.) Hence 1 and 2 only.

**Q.75 (CDS 2017-II)** — The base year of India's new GDP series was shifted from 2004-05 to
a) 2007-08  b) 2008-09  c) 2010-11  d) 2011-12
**Correct Answer:** d) 2011-12
**Explanation:** GDP is measured at "constant prices" using a **base year** to strip out the effect of inflation, so that growth reflects real changes in output. Periodically the base year is updated to a more recent, representative year. In 2015 India's Central Statistics Office revised the National Accounts base year from 2004-05 to **2011-12**, and also began headlining **GDP at market prices** and **Gross Value Added (GVA)**, bringing the methodology closer to international (UN System of National Accounts) standards.

**Q.112 (CDS 2018-II)** — Per updated World Bank data for 2017, India is the sixth biggest economy by GDP. Which one is not ahead of India?
a) Japan  b) UK  c) France  d) Germany
**Correct Answer:** c) France
**Explanation:** This tests the ranking of the world's largest economies by nominal GDP around 2017. At that point the order ran United States, China, Japan, Germany, UK and then India — meaning India had just **overtaken France** to claim sixth place. So Japan, Germany and the UK were still ahead, but **France was not** (c). *(A current-affairs/data item; relative rankings of the UK, France and India have shifted back and forth since.)*

**Q.43 (CDS 2019-II)** — Indexation is a method associated with which of the following?
a) Controlling inflation  b) Nominal GDP estimation  c) Measurement of savings rate  d) Fixing of wage compensation
**Correct Answer:** d) Fixing of wage compensation
**Explanation:** **Indexation** is the practice of automatically adjusting a money value to a price index (usually the CPI) so that its real (inflation-adjusted) value is preserved. Its commonest use is in **fixing wage/salary compensation** — for example, the Dearness Allowance paid to government employees and pensioners is revised in line with the consumer price index, protecting their purchasing power as prices rise. It does not by itself *control* inflation (a) — it only neutralises inflation's effect on incomes — and it is not a technique for estimating nominal GDP (b) or the savings rate (c).

**Q.4 (CDS 2020-II)** — [Tax receipts as % of GDP: 1. Corporation Tax 2. Income Tax 3. GST 4. Union Excise Duties.] Which is the correct descending order of these tax receipts as a percentage of GDP?
a) 1, 2, 3, 4  b) 1, 3, 2, 4  c) 3, 2, 1, 4  d) 2, 4, 3, 1
**Correct Answer:** b) 1, 3, 2, 4  *(data-based ordering — verify against the year's budget)*
**Explanation:** This is a data-recall item from the 2019-20 Union Budget. Among the major taxes, **corporation tax** (tax on company profits) was the single largest head, followed by **GST** (the unified indirect tax), then **personal income tax**, with **Union excise duties** (now levied mainly on petroleum products after most goods moved to GST) the smallest of the four — giving the descending order 1, 3, 2, 4. *(Exact ranks vary slightly year to year as collections shift, so confirm with the relevant budget document.)*

**(CDS 2020-II)** — As per the World Bank Ease of Doing Business ranking, India improved from 142 (2014) to 63 (2019). In which parameter did India's rank deteriorate?
a) Ease of starting a business  b) Getting electricity  c) Registering property  d) Paying taxes
**Correct Answer:** c) Registering property  *(verify)*
**Explanation:** The World Bank's Ease of Doing Business index scored countries on ten parameters (starting a business, getting electricity, paying taxes, enforcing contracts, registering property, etc.). India's overall jump from 142 to 63 was driven by big gains in areas like construction permits, getting electricity, paying taxes and resolving insolvency. The one parameter where India lagged or slipped over the period was **registering property**, where cumbersome land-record and title-transfer processes kept India's rank low. *(A data-recall item worth confirming against the original key.)*

**(CDS 2020-II)** — Which statement about the National Food Security Act is not correct?
a) The Act was enacted in 2013.  b) The Act was rolled out in 2014.  c) It legally entitles 67% of the population to highly subsidised foodgrains.  d) The Act is not being implemented in all the States/UTs.
**Correct Answer:** d)
**Explanation:** The **National Food Security Act (NFSA), 2013** converted access to subsidised foodgrains from a welfare scheme into a **legal right**. It covers up to 75% of the rural and 50% of the urban population — about **two-thirds (67%) of India's people** — who get rice/wheat/coarse grains at highly subsidised prices (₹3/2/1 per kg) through the Public Distribution System. By the time of this question it had been **rolled out across all States and Union Territories** (Tamil Nadu being among the last). So statement d — that it is *not* implemented everywhere — is the incorrect one; the enactment year (2013), roll-out and 67% coverage figures are all correct.

**(CDS 2020-II)** — Which statement about the Indian economy during 2019-20 is not correct?
a) There was deceleration in the growth rate.  b) Tax revenue grew sluggishly relative to Budget Estimates.  c) The fiscal deficit as a percentage of GDP was as per the Budget Estimates.  d) Non-tax revenue registered considerably higher growth.
**Correct Answer:** c)
**Explanation:** 2019-20 was a year of slowing growth (GDP growth fell to around 4%), and with output and consumption weak, **tax collections undershot the budgeted targets**, while one-off receipts (like the large RBI dividend transfer) lifted **non-tax revenue**. Because tax revenue fell short, the government could not hold the deficit to plan: the **fiscal deficit overshot the Budget Estimate**, slipping to roughly 3.8% of GDP against the budgeted ~3.3%. So statement c — that the fiscal deficit stayed "as per the Budget Estimates" — is not correct; the deceleration, sluggish tax revenue and higher non-tax revenue (a, b, d) are all accurate.

**Q.1 (CDS 2021-I)** — Which statement is not correct?
a) Real GDP is calculated by valuing outputs of different years at common prices.  b) Potential GDP is the real GDP the economy would produce with fully employed resources.  c) Nominal GDP is calculated by valuing outputs of different years at constant prices.  d) Real GDP per capita is real GDP divided by population.
**Correct Answer:** c)
**Explanation:** The key distinction is between **nominal** and **real** GDP. **Nominal GDP** values output at **current (each year's own) prices**, so it rises both when output grows and when prices rise. **Real GDP** values output at **constant (base-year) prices**, removing inflation so that changes reflect genuine growth (a, correctly describes real GDP). Statement c reverses this — it wrongly says nominal GDP uses *constant* prices — so it is the incorrect statement. **Potential GDP** (the full-employment level of output, b) and **real GDP per capita** (real GDP ÷ population, d) are both correctly defined.

**Q.26 (CDS 2021-I)** — A situation where equilibrium real GDP falls short of potential GDP is known as
a) Recessionary gap  b) Inflationary gap  c) Demand-side inflation  d) Supply-side inflation
**Correct Answer:** a) Recessionary gap
**Explanation:** Potential GDP is the output an economy would produce with all resources fully and efficiently employed; actual (equilibrium) real GDP is what it actually produces. The difference is the **output gap**. When actual output lies **below** potential, the economy has idle capacity and unemployed labour, and this shortfall — caused by deficient aggregate demand — is the **recessionary (or deflationary) gap** (a). Its opposite is the **inflationary gap** (b), where demand pushes actual output *above* potential and overheats the economy. Options c and d name *types of inflation by cause* (demand-pull vs cost-push), not the output-gap condition described.

**Q.49 (CDS 2021-II)** — Which statement about social-sector spending in India during 2014-19 (States + Union together) is true?
a) Education spending was 5% of GDP.  b) Health spending was 4% of social-services expenditure.  c) There was stagnation in education spending as a percent of GDP.  d) Health-sector spending was 10% of total expenditure.
**Correct Answer:** c) There was a stagnation in the spending on education as a percent of GDP
**Explanation:** "Social-sector spending" covers government outlays on education, health, social security, etc. A recurring theme in Economic Surveys of this period is that, although the absolute amounts rose, **education expenditure stayed roughly flat at about 2.8–3% of GDP** — well short of the long-recommended 6% — so it **stagnated as a share of GDP** (c). The other options quote incorrect figures: education was not 5% of GDP (a), and the health figures in (b) and (d) are misstated (public health spending was only around 1–1.5% of GDP).

**Q.67 (CDS 2023-I)** — Which indicator is included in the National Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI)?
a) Years of schooling  b) Literacy rate  c) Life expectancy  d) Per capita income
**Correct Answer:** a) Years of schooling
**Explanation:** The **National MPI**, prepared by NITI Aayog (modelled on the global MPI of UNDP/OPHI), measures poverty not by income but by **multiple deprivations** across three dimensions — **Health, Education and Standard of Living** — captured by twelve indicators. Under the **Education** dimension it uses **years of schooling** and **school attendance** (a). It deliberately avoids income-based measures, so **per capita income (d)** is excluded; and it uses *years of schooling* rather than the aggregate *literacy rate (b)*, and *child & maternal nutrition/mortality* rather than *life expectancy (c)*.

**Q.66 (CDS 2024-I)** — 1. The GDP deflator captures the average price of an unchanging basket of commodities. 2. The GDP deflator can measure real GDP but not the inflation rate.
a) 1 only  b) 2 only  c) Both 1 and 2  d) Neither 1 nor 2
**Correct Answer:** d) Neither 1 nor 2
**Explanation:** The **GDP deflator** is the ratio of nominal GDP to real GDP (×100); it is the broadest price index because it covers **every good and service in the economy's current output**, and that basket **changes every year** as production changes. Statement 1 confuses it with the **CPI**, which tracks a *fixed* basket of consumer goods — so 1 is wrong. Statement 2 is also wrong: the deflator is itself a measure of the price level, so it **can be used to compute the inflation rate** (the percentage change in the deflator). Since both statements are false, the answer is "neither."

**Q.73 (CDS 2024-I)** — Per capita GDP depends upon: 1. Proportion of population in the working age. 2. Work participation rate. 3. Per-worker productivity.
a) 3 only  b) 1 and 3 only  c) 1 and 2 only  d) 1, 2 and 3
**Correct Answer:** d) 1, 2 and 3
**Explanation:** Per capita GDP (output per person) can be decomposed into a chain of ratios: GDP per person = (working-age population ÷ total population) × (workers ÷ working-age population) × (GDP ÷ workers). In words, it depends on the **share of people of working age (1)** — the demographic-dividend factor — the **work participation rate (2)** — how many of them actually work — and the **output each worker produces, i.e. productivity (3)**. All three multiply together, so all three matter (d).

**Q.75 (CDS 2024-I)** — Which statement is not correct for National Income Accounting in India?
a) Imports are subtracted in calculating GDP.  b) Net factor payments earned from abroad are included in Gross Domestic Product.  c) Purchase/sale of second-hand goods are not included in GDP.  d) Inventories are included in Gross Domestic Capital Formation.
**Correct Answer:** b)
**Explanation:** GDP measures the value of goods and services produced **within a country's domestic territory** in a year. Two adjustments are routine: imports are subtracted (they are produced abroad, a — correct), and **second-hand goods are excluded** because their value was already counted when first produced (c — correct); changes in **inventories (unsold stock) count as investment**, hence part of capital formation (d — correct). Statement b is the error: **net factor income from abroad is added to GDP to arrive at GNP (Gross National Product)** — it is *not* part of GDP itself, which is a strictly "domestic" concept.

**Q.74 (CDS 2025-II)** — About innovation and R&D in India: 1. India's Global Innovation Index rank improved significantly between 2015 and 2024. 2. Effective capital expenditure as % of GDP increased from 2020-21 to 2023-24.
a) 1 only  b) 2 only  c) Both 1 and 2  d) Neither 1 nor 2
**Correct Answer:** c) Both 1 and 2
**Explanation:** The **Global Innovation Index (GII)**, published annually by the World Intellectual Property Organization, ranks economies on innovation inputs and outputs; India climbed dramatically from around 81st in 2015 into the 39–40 range by 2023-24, reflecting its growing R&D, start-up and patent ecosystem (statement 1 correct). Separately, the government's post-pandemic strategy emphasised a **capital-expenditure push** — "effective capital expenditure" (capex plus grants-in-aid for creation of capital assets) **rose as a share of GDP** from 2020-21 to 2023-24 to crowd in investment (statement 2 correct). The answer options only test statements 1 and 2, both of which hold.

**Q.76 (CDS 2025-II)** — 1. Effective capital expenditure as % of GDP increased from 2020-21 to 2023-24. 2. Expenditure on revenue account as % of GDP increased from 2020-21 to 2023-24.
a) 1 only  b) 2 only  c) Both 1 and 2  d) Neither 1 nor 2
**Correct Answer:** a) 1 only
**Explanation:** Government spending splits into **capital expenditure** (which creates assets — roads, railways, capital grants) and **revenue expenditure** (recurring spending — salaries, interest, subsidies). The deliberate fiscal strategy after COVID-19 was to **raise capex and rein in revenue spending**, both as shares of GDP, to improve the *quality* of expenditure and boost growth. So **effective capital expenditure rose (statement 1, correct)**, while **revenue expenditure as a share of GDP fell** rather than rose (statement 2, incorrect). Hence 1 only.

**Q.118 (CDS 2025-II)** — Which is a good statistic to evaluate where an economy stands in the financial cycle?
a) Tax/GDP Ratio  b) Fiscal Deficit/GDP Ratio  c) Household Consumption/GDP Ratio  d) Credit/GDP Ratio
**Correct Answer:** d) Credit/GDP Ratio
**Explanation:** The **financial cycle** refers to the self-reinforcing swings in credit and asset prices — booms in which lending and leverage build up, followed by busts. The standard early-warning indicator for this, used by the Bank for International Settlements and central banks, is the **credit-to-GDP ratio** and its deviation from trend (the "credit-to-GDP gap"): a sharply rising ratio signals an unsustainable credit boom and financial-stability risk. The other ratios describe the *fiscal* stance (tax/GDP, fiscal-deficit/GDP) or *demand* composition (consumption/GDP), not the financial cycle.

## Theories & Indicators of Growth

**Q.91 (CDS 2016-II)** — According to the Classical Theory of Employment, deviations from full employment are
a) purely temporary in nature  b) permanent in nature  c) imaginary situations  d) normal situations
**Correct Answer:** a) purely temporary in nature
**Explanation:** Classical economists (Adam Smith, Ricardo, J.B. Say) believed in **Say's Law** — "supply creates its own demand" — and held that flexible wages, prices and interest rates would always steer the economy back to **full employment**. In their view, any unemployment was a short-lived disequilibrium that self-corrects (wages fall, hiring resumes), so deviations from full employment are **purely temporary** (a). It was Keynes who later challenged this, arguing that an economy could get *stuck* in prolonged under-employment because of deficient aggregate demand.

**Q.92 (CDS 2016-II)** — Capital deepening refers to
a) going for more fixed capital per worker  b) emphasis on social overhead capital  c) constant capital-output ratio  d) increasing capital-output ratio
**Correct Answer:** a) going for more fixed capital per worker
**Explanation:** **Capital deepening** means raising the amount of **capital (machines, tools, equipment) available per worker** — equipping each worker with more/better capital, which typically raises labour productivity and wages. It is contrasted with **capital widening**, where capital grows only in step with the workforce, keeping capital-per-worker constant. Options c and d describe the capital-output ratio (how much capital is needed per unit of output), which is a different concept, and b refers to infrastructure (social overhead capital), not deepening.

**Q.90 (CDS 2017-I)** — The Twelfth Plan focused on inclusive growth. Which were considered challenges for inclusiveness? 1. Poverty 2. Group inequality 3. Regional imbalance 4. Unemployment.
a) 1, 3 and 4 only  b) 1, 2, 3 and 4  c) 1, 2 and 4 only  d) 2 and 3 only
**Correct Answer:** b) 1, 2, 3 and 4
**Explanation:** The Twelfth Five-Year Plan (2012-17), themed "Faster, More Inclusive and Sustainable Growth," argued that growth is "inclusive" only if it reaches all sections and regions. It explicitly identified the barriers to inclusiveness as **poverty** (income deprivation), **group inequality** (gaps across caste/religion/gender groups), **regional imbalance** (rich vs lagging states) and **unemployment** (lack of productive jobs) — all four (b). Tackling these together, not just raising the headline growth rate, was the plan's stated aim.

**Q.28 (CDS 2017-II)** — The phenomenon of demographic dividend relates to
a) a sharp decline in total population  b) an increase in working-age population  c) a decline in infant mortality rate  d) an increase in sex ratio
**Correct Answer:** b) an increase in working-age population
**Explanation:** The **demographic dividend** is the boost to economic growth that can occur when a country's **age structure shifts so that the working-age population (15-59/64) grows faster than the dependent population** (children and the elderly). With proportionally more earners and fewer dependents, savings, investment and output per head can rise — *provided* the extra workers are educated, healthy and employed. India is currently in this window. It is about the **age composition**, not a fall in total population (a), and while falling infant mortality (c) helps drive the transition, the dividend itself is the rise in the working-age share.

**Q.29 (CDS 2017-II)** — Arrange in sequence: 1. Mahalanobis Model 2. Plan Holiday 3. Rolling Plan.
a) 1, 2, 3  b) 3, 2, 1  c) 2, 3, 1  d) 1, 3, 2
**Correct Answer:** a) 1, 2, 3
**Explanation:** These are landmarks in Indian planning history. The **Mahalanobis Model** underpinned the **Second Five-Year Plan (1956)**, emphasising heavy industry. The **"Plan Holiday"** was the three-year break (**1966-69**) after the Third Plan, forced by wars (1962, 1965), drought and a foreign-exchange crisis, during which annual plans replaced the five-year cycle. The **Rolling Plan** concept was adopted in **1978** by the Janata government (an annually revised plan). In chronological order, therefore: Mahalanobis (1956) → Plan Holiday (1966) → Rolling Plan (1978) = 1, 2, 3.

**Q.107 (CDS 2018-I)** — Which statement about the DDU-GKY is not correct?
a) It is a placement-linked skill-training programme exclusively for rural girls.  b) It aims to convert India's demographic surplus into a demographic dividend.  c) It aims to benefit more than 55 million poor rural folk.  d) It is a generational poverty-alleviation programme.
**Correct Answer:** a)
**Explanation:** The **Deen Dayal Upadhyaya Grameen Kaushalya Yojana (DDU-GKY)**, launched in 2014 under the Ministry of Rural Development, is the skilling arm of the National Rural Livelihoods Mission. It provides **placement-linked skill training to rural poor youth aged 15-35** to help convert the country's young population (its "demographic surplus") into a productive **demographic dividend** (b) and to lift families out of poverty across generations (c, d). Statement a is wrong because the scheme is for **rural youth of both sexes — not "exclusively rural girls"** (though it does mandate a high share of women, SC/ST and minority candidates).

**Q.54 (CDS 2018-II)** — The natural rate of unemployment hypothesis was advocated by
a) Milton Friedman  b) A. W. Phillips  c) J. M. Keynes  d) R. G. Lipsey
**Correct Answer:** a) Milton Friedman
**Explanation:** The **natural rate of unemployment** is the rate that persists even when the labour market is in equilibrium — the sum of frictional and structural unemployment, with cyclical unemployment at zero. **Milton Friedman** (and independently Edmund Phelps) advanced this hypothesis in the late 1960s, arguing that attempts to push unemployment *below* this natural rate by stimulating demand only produce accelerating inflation, not lasting job gains — demolishing the idea of a permanent inflation-unemployment trade-off. **A. W. Phillips** (b) gave the original Phillips Curve, which Friedman's hypothesis modified.

**Q.25 (CDS 2019-I)** — When some goods or productive factors are completely fixed in amount regardless of price, the supply curve is
a) horizontal  b) downward sloping to the right  c) vertical  d) upward sloping to the right
**Correct Answer:** c) vertical
**Explanation:** A supply curve shows how quantity supplied responds to price. If the quantity **cannot change no matter what the price** — for example, the supply of land, or of a unique original painting — supply is **perfectly inelastic**, and a perfectly inelastic supply curve is a **vertical line** (c) at the fixed quantity. (A horizontal curve, a, means perfectly *elastic* supply; an upward-sloping curve, d, is the normal case where higher prices bring more supply.)

**Q.37 (CDS 2019-I)** — A market situation where many firms sell similar but not identical products is termed
a) perfect competition  b) imperfect competition  c) monopolistic competition  d) oligopoly
**Correct Answer:** c) monopolistic competition
**Explanation:** **Monopolistic competition** is a market with **many sellers offering differentiated products** — similar but not identical (think toothpaste, soap, restaurants). Because each product is slightly different (through branding, quality, packaging), every firm has a little pricing power, yet free entry keeps long-run profits low. This differs from **perfect competition** (a), where products are *identical*; from **monopoly**; and from **oligopoly** (d), where only a *few* firms dominate.

**Q.40 (CDS 2019-I)** — According to John Maynard Keynes, employment depends upon
a) aggregate demand  b) aggregate supply  c) effective demand  d) rate of interest
**Correct Answer:** c) effective demand
**Explanation:** Keynes's central insight (in *The General Theory*, 1936) was the **principle of effective demand**: the level of employment in an economy is determined by the point where **aggregate demand equals aggregate supply** — that equilibrium point is "effective demand." If effective demand is too low, firms produce less and employ fewer workers, leaving the economy stuck below full employment. This is why he advocated boosting demand (e.g. through government spending) in a slump. While aggregate demand (a) is part of the story, the precise Keynesian term is **effective demand** (c).

**Q.80 (CDS 2019-II)** — What is meant by Demographic Dividend?
a) A rise in economic growth due to a higher share of working-age people  b) A rise in literacy due to educational institutions  c) A rise in living standards due to alternative livelihoods  d) A rise in the employment ratio due to government policies
**Correct Answer:** a)
**Explanation:** The **demographic dividend** is the **acceleration in economic growth that results when the working-age share of the population rises** relative to dependents (children and elderly). More workers per dependent means higher production, savings and investment per head — a one-time growth bonus during the demographic transition, which a country can reap only if it educates, keeps healthy and employs that workforce. The other options describe literacy, livelihoods or employment programmes, which are not the definition of the dividend.

**Q.87 (CDS 2019-II)** — The value of the slope of a normal demand curve is
a) positive  b) negative  c) zero  d) infinity
**Correct Answer:** b) negative
**Explanation:** The **law of demand** states that, other things equal, as the price of a good falls the quantity demanded rises (and vice versa). Plotted with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal, this inverse relationship gives a curve that **slopes downward from left to right**, i.e. it has a **negative slope** (b). A zero slope (c) would mean quantity is unaffected by price, and an infinite slope would be a vertical curve — neither describes a normal demand curve.

**Q.117 (CDS 2020-I)** — Match: A. Perfect competition  B. Monopoly  C. Monopolistic competition  D. Oligopoly | 1. One producer selling one commodity  2. Few producers selling similar/almost similar products  3. Many producers selling differentiated products  4. Many producers selling similar products.
a) A-4 B-3 C-1 D-2  b) A-4 B-1 C-3 D-2  c) A-2 B-1 C-3 D-4  d) A-2 B-3 C-1 D-4
**Correct Answer:** b) A-4 B-1 C-3 D-2
**Explanation:** Market structures are classified by the **number of sellers and the nature of the product**. **Perfect competition** has *many* sellers of an *identical (homogeneous)* product → "many producers selling similar products" (4). **Monopoly** is a *single* seller of a product with no close substitute → "one producer, one commodity" (1). **Monopolistic competition** has *many* sellers of *differentiated* products → (3). **Oligopoly** has a *few* large, interdependent sellers → "few producers selling similar/almost similar products" (2). Hence A-4, B-1, C-3, D-2.

**Q.34 (CDS 2020-II)** — Normally, there will not be a shift in the demand curve when
a) price of the commodity falls  b) consumers want to buy more at any given price  c) average income rises  d) population grows
**Correct Answer:** a) price of a commodity falls
**Explanation:** A crucial distinction in microeconomics is between a **movement along** a demand curve and a **shift of** the whole curve. A change in the good's **own price** moves you *along* the existing curve (a change in quantity demanded) — so a fall in the commodity's price does **not shift** the curve (a). The curve itself **shifts** only when a *non-price determinant* changes: a rise in average income (c), population growth (d), or a change in tastes such that consumers want more at every price (b) all move the entire demand curve.

**Q.35 (CDS 2020-II)** — A market with a large number of firms, a homogeneous product, infinite elasticity of demand for an individual firm and no price control is
a) oligopoly  b) imperfect competition  c) monopolistic competition  d) perfect competition
**Correct Answer:** d) perfect competition
**Explanation:** These are exactly the defining assumptions of **perfect competition**: a **very large number of buyers and sellers**, a **homogeneous (identical) product**, free entry and exit, perfect information, and — crucially — each firm is a **price-taker** facing a **perfectly elastic (horizontal) demand curve**, with **no control over price**. Because no single firm can influence the market price, it simply accepts the price set by overall demand and supply. The other structures all involve product differentiation, few sellers, or some price-setting power.

**Q.33 (CDS 2021-I)** — When did the East India Company lose its monopoly of the China trade?
a) 1813  b) 1833  c) 1838  d) 1860
**Correct Answer:** b) 1833
**Explanation:** The British Parliament periodically renewed the East India Company's charter and progressively stripped its commercial privileges. The **Charter Act of 1813** ended the Company's monopoly over trade with India (except tea and the China trade). The **Charter Act of 1833** then abolished its **remaining monopoly — the lucrative tea trade and trade with China** — turning the Company into a purely administrative body. Hence 1833 (b). *(A history item.)*

**Q.47 (CDS 2021-II)** — Which may lead to a movement along the demand curve of a commodity?
a) Change in its price  b) Change in price of other commodities  c) Change in consumer income  d) Change in tastes and preferences
**Correct Answer:** a) Change in its price
**Explanation:** Only a change in the good's **own price** causes a **movement along** its demand curve — that is the very relationship the curve plots (price against quantity demanded). The other factors are **non-price determinants**: a change in the price of related goods (substitutes/complements, b), in consumer income (c), or in tastes and preferences (d) all **shift the entire demand curve** to a new position rather than moving along it.

**Q.70 (CDS 2021-II)** — Which is a typical example of monopolistic competition?
a) Retail vegetable markets  b) Market for soaps  c) Indian Railways  d) Labour market for software engineers
**Correct Answer:** b) Market for soaps
**Explanation:** Monopolistic competition needs **many sellers offering differentiated (branded) products**. **Q.76 (CDS 2024-I)** — With a normal downward-sloping demand curve and a fully elastic (horizontal) supply curve, an exogenous decrease in demand leads to

**Q.97 (CDS 2024-II)** — Which statement about a firm's equilibrium in perfect competition is not correct?

**Correct Answer:** d) The marginal cost decreases at the equilibrium output
**Explanation:** A perfectly competitive firm maximises profit where **price = marginal cost (MC)**, and for that point to be a genuine maximum, **MC must be rising** (cutting the horizontal price line from below) — so statement d, that MC is *decreasing* at equilibrium, is **incorrect**. **Q.71 (CDS 2025-II)** — Which pair of firm and feature is not correctly matched?

## WPI, CPI & Inflation

**Q.100 (CDS 2017-I)** — Which index is now used by the Reserve Bank of India to measure the rate of inflation?
a) NASDAQ Index  b) BSE Index  c) Consumer Price Index  d) Wholesale Price Index
**Correct Answer:** c) Consumer Price Index
**Explanation:** Following the recommendations of the Urjit Patel Committee and the adoption of a formal **inflation-targeting** framework in 2016, the RBI's nominal anchor became the **Consumer Price Index (CPI-Combined)** — the price index that reflects what households actually pay at the retail level. The RBI's mandate is to keep CPI inflation at **4% within a ±2% band**. Earlier the **Wholesale Price Index (WPI, d)** was the headline measure, but WPI excludes services and reflects wholesale not retail prices, so CPI was judged a better gauge of consumer welfare. **Q.69 (CDS 2021-II)** — Match: A. Lorenz curve  B. Phillips curve  C. Engel curve  D. Laffer curve | 1. Inflation and employment  2. Tax rates and tax revenue  3. Inequality in income/wealth distribution  4. Income and proportion of expenditure on food.
a) A-3 B-4 C-1 D-2  b) A-2 B-1 C-4 D-3  c) A-3 B-1 C-4 D-2  d) A-2 B-4 C-1 D-3
**Correct Answer:** c) A-3 B-1 C-4 D-2
**Explanation:** Each is a famous named relationship. The **Lorenz curve** plots the cumulative share of income/wealth against the cumulative share of population, measuring **inequality** (3). The **Phillips curve** shows the inverse short-run relationship between **inflation and unemployment/employment** (1). **Engel's curve/law** states that as income rises, the **proportion of income spent on food falls** (4). The **Laffer curve** depicts how **tax revenue** first rises then falls as the **tax rate** climbs, implying an optimal rate (2). Hence A-3, B-1, C-4, D-2.

**Correct Answer:** c) Rubber products
**Explanation:** The **Index of Eight Core Industries** tracks the output of the foundational industries that feed the rest of the economy and together carry about **40% weight in the Index of Industrial Production (IIP)**. **Q.20 (CDS 2023-I)** — Which situation can lead to inflation?
a) Rapid growth of aggregate demand outweighing supply  b) Sluggish growth of aggregate demand  c) Reduction in money supply  d) Higher levels of unemployment
**Correct Answer:** a) Rapid growth of aggregate demand outweighing supply
**Explanation:** Inflation — a sustained rise in the general price level — has two classic drivers. **Demand-pull inflation** occurs when **aggregate demand grows faster than the economy's ability to supply goods**, so "too much money chases too few goods" and prices are bid up (a). The other options are *disinflationary*: weak demand (b), a smaller money supply (c) and high unemployment (d) all reduce spending pressure and tend to lower, not raise, prices.

**Q.65 (CDS 2023-I)** — Which measure can the Government use to combat inflation?
a) Increasing non-plan expenditure on defence, police, etc.  b) Providing more export subsidies  c) Increasing the rate of interest on savings and fixed deposits  d) Reducing the cash reserve ratio (CRR)
**Correct Answer:** c) Increasing the rate of interest on savings and fixed deposits
**Explanation:** To fight inflation, policymakers try to **reduce excess demand/money in circulation**. **Raising interest rates on savings and fixed deposits** rewards saving and discourages spending and borrowing, pulling money out of the spending stream and cooling demand (c). The other options would *worsen* inflation: higher government spending (a) and export subsidies (b) add to demand, and **cutting the CRR** (d) releases more money for banks to lend — all expansionary.

**Q.32 (CDS 2023-II)** — Effects of devaluation/depreciation of currency: 1. Increase in imports and decrease in exports. 2. Increase in exports and decrease in imports. 3. Increase in domestic inflation. 4. Decrease in domestic inflation.
a) 1 and 3 only  b) 1 and 4 only  c) 2 and 3 only  d) 3 only
**Correct Answer:** c) 2 and 3 only
**Explanation:** When a currency **depreciates/devalues**, each unit of foreign currency buys more of it — so the country's goods become **cheaper for foreigners (exports rise)** and foreign goods become **dearer at home (imports fall)** — statement 2 (this is the intended trade-balance improvement). At the same time, **imported inputs and goods cost more in local currency**, pushing up domestic prices — so devaluation tends to **raise domestic inflation** (statement 3). Statements 1 and 4 are the opposite of these and are wrong. Hence 2 and 3 only.

**Q.67 (CDS 2025-I)** — Core inflation is

**Q.107 (CDS 2016-II)** — In India, the term "hot money" refers to

**Q.66 (CDS 2023-I)** — The sustained decrease in the general price level is called

**Correct Answer:** a) deflation
**Explanation:** **Deflation** is a **sustained fall in the general price level** (negative inflation); it can be harmful because falling prices make consumers delay purchases and raise the real burden of debt. Don't confuse it with: **stagflation** (b) — high inflation together with stagnant growth and high unemployment; **devaluation** (c) — a deliberate reduction in a currency's external value; and **recession** (d) — a sustained fall in output/GDP, not prices.

**Q.30 (CDS 2023-II)** — Which government actions would contract the money supply? 1. Purchase of Treasury Bills by the central bank from the public. 2. Sale of Treasury Bills by the central bank to the public. 3. Sale of foreign exchange by the central bank. 4. Purchase of foreign exchange by the central bank.
a) 1 and 4 only  b) 1 and 3 only  c) 2 and 3 only  d) 2 only
**Correct Answer:** c) 2 and 3 only
**Explanation:** The money supply contracts when the central bank **takes rupees out of circulation**. When the RBI **sells Treasury Bills to the public (2)**, buyers pay rupees to the RBI, draining money from the system. When the RBI **sells foreign exchange (3)**, buyers again hand over rupees, again absorbing money. **Q.95 (CDS 2024-II)** — Which is not included in the Capital Account of the Balance of Payments of a country?

**Correct Answer:** c) Invisibles
**Explanation:** The **Balance of Payments** has two main parts. The **Current Account** records trade in goods, plus **"invisibles"** — services (software, tourism), income (interest, dividends) and transfers (remittances). **Q.66 (CDS 2025-I)** — How can currency depreciation stimulate an increase in net exports? 1. By reducing export costs. 2. By reducing import prices.

**Q.101 (CDS 2017-I)** — The Most Favoured Nation (MFN) clause under the WTO is based on the principle of
a) non-discrimination between nations  b) discrimination between nations  c) differential treatment between locals and foreigners  d) uniform tariff across commodities
**Correct Answer:** a) non-discrimination between nations
**Explanation:** The **Most Favoured Nation (MFN)** principle is a cornerstone of the WTO: it requires that any trade advantage (a tariff cut, a market opening) a member grants to **one** country must be **extended equally to all** WTO members. In other words, you cannot discriminate between your trading partners — hence the principle of **non-discrimination among nations** (a). (The related "National Treatment" rule deals with treating foreign and domestic goods alike *within* a country — different from MFN, so c is wrong.)

**Q.102 (CDS 2017-I)** — The Goods and Services Tax is not a
a) gross value tax  b) value-added tax  c) consumption tax  d) destination-based tax
**Correct Answer:** a) gross value tax
**Explanation:** GST is designed as a **value-added tax (VAT)** — at each stage a business pays tax only on the **value it adds**, claiming credit (input-tax credit) for tax already paid on its inputs, which avoids "tax-on-tax" cascading. It is **consumption-based** (borne ultimately by the final consumer) and **destination-based** (revenue accrues to the state where the good/service is consumed, not produced). What it is **not** is a **"gross value tax"** (a) — a tax on the gross value at every stage with no input credit, which is exactly the cascading system GST was created to eliminate.

**Q.52 (CDS 2020-I)** — The Cash Reserve Ratio refers to
a) the share of NDTL banks hold as liquid assets  b) the share of NDTL banks hold as balances with the RBI  c) the share of NDTL banks hold as part of their cash reserves  d) the ratio of cash holding to reserves of banks
**Correct Answer:** b) the share of Net Demand and Time Liabilities that banks have to hold as balances with the RBI
**Explanation:** The **Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR)** is the percentage of a bank's **Net Demand and Time Liabilities (NDTL — essentially its deposits)** that it must keep **as cash balances with the RBI** (earning no interest). By raising or lowering the CRR the RBI reduces or increases the funds banks have available to lend, making it a tool of monetary control. **Q.21 (CDS 2023-I)** — Which policy mix raises the interest rate unambiguously and leads to currency appreciation?
a) Expansionary fiscal and monetary policy  b) Contractionary fiscal and monetary policy  c) Contractionary fiscal and expansionary monetary policy  d) Contractionary monetary and expansionary fiscal policy
**Correct Answer:** d) Contractionary monetary policy and expansionary fiscal policy
**Explanation:** Two forces push interest rates up. **Contractionary monetary policy** (less money supply / higher policy rates) directly raises rates. **Expansionary fiscal policy** (more government spending/borrowing) raises the demand for loanable funds, "crowding in" higher rates too. When **both act together (d)**, interest rates rise **unambiguously**. Higher domestic rates attract foreign capital inflows, increasing demand for the currency and causing it to **appreciate**. The other mixes have offsetting effects on rates, so the direction is not unambiguous.

**Q.31 (CDS 2023-II)** — Which are included in Narrow Money (M1)? 1. Currency with the public 2. Demand deposits 3. Other deposits with the RBI 4. Bankers' deposits with the RBI.
a) 1, 2 and 4  b) 1 and 2 only  c) 1, 2 and 3  d) 3 and 4 only
**Correct Answer:** c) 1, 2 and 3
**Explanation:** The RBI defines money supply in graded measures. **Narrow Money (M1)** = **currency with the public + demand deposits with banks + "other deposits" with the RBI** (1, 2, 3) — the most *liquid* forms of money usable for transactions on demand. **Bankers' deposits with the RBI (4)** are deliberately **excluded** from M1 (they are part of "reserve money," not money in public hands). **Q.30 (CDS 2024-I)** — Regarding monetary-policy instruments: 1. The Standing Deposit Facility (SDF) rate was introduced in April 2022. 2. The SDF rate replaced the fixed reverse repo rate as the floor of the LAF corridor.
a) 1 only  b) 2 only  c) Both 1 and 2  d) Neither 1 nor 2
**Correct Answer:** c) Both 1 and 2
**Explanation:** The RBI manages liquidity through the **Liquidity Adjustment Facility (LAF) corridor**: the **repo rate** is the policy rate in the middle, the **MSF (marginal standing facility)** is the ceiling, and the floor is where banks park surplus cash. **Q.2 (CDS 2021-I)** — The mismatch in the regional or occupational pattern of job vacancies and the pattern of worker availability results in
a) Structural unemployment  b) Disguised unemployment  c) Altered unemployment  d) Cyclical unemployment
**Correct Answer:** a) Structural unemployment

**Explanation:** The **Economic Advisory Council to the Prime Minister (EAC-PM)** is an independent body that advises the PM on economic matters. At the time of this question it was **chaired by the economist Bibek Debroy** (b). *(A current-affairs item.)*

**Q.112 (CDS 2020-I)** — Formulation of policy on Intellectual Property Rights (IPRs) is the responsibility of

**Q.75 (CDS 2020-II)** — Which statement about the appointment of Members of Parliamentary Committees is correct?

**Correct Answer:** d)
**Explanation:** Members of **Parliamentary Committees** are constituted in two ways — they are **elected by the House** (through proportional representation by single transferable vote, on a motion moved and adopted) **or nominated by the Speaker (Lok Sabha)/Chairman (Rajya Sabha)** — depending on the committee. Option d captures this dual mechanism correctly. *(A polity item.)*

**Q.6 (CDS 2021-I)** — The percentage by which the money the borrower pays back exceeds the money borrowed is called
a) Bank rate  b) Nominal interest rate  c) Real interest rate  d) Terms of credit
**Correct Answer:** b) Nominal interest rate
**Explanation:** The **nominal interest rate** is simply the **stated percentage by which the amount repaid exceeds the amount borrowed**, in money terms, before adjusting for inflation (b). The **real interest rate (c)** is the nominal rate *minus inflation* (the true increase in purchasing power). The **bank rate (a)** is a specific central-bank lending rate, and **terms of credit (d)** is the broader set of loan conditions (rate, collateral, documentation, repayment).

**Q.51 (CDS 2021-I)** — Which motion cannot be made while introducing an ordinary Bill in Parliament?
a) That the Bill be taken into consideration  b) That the Bill be circulated for eliciting public opinion  c) That the Bill be referred to a Select Committee  d) That the Bill be referred to a Joint Committee of the House without the concurrence of the other House
**Correct Answer:** d)
**Explanation:** When a Bill is introduced, the member-in-charge can move that it be **taken into consideration (a)**, **referred to a Select Committee of the House (c)**, or **circulated to elicit public opinion (b)**. A Bill **cannot** be referred to a **Joint Committee of both Houses without the concurrence (agreement) of the other House** — a joint committee involves both Houses, so the other House must consent (d is therefore the motion that cannot be made unilaterally). *(A polity item.)*

a) 1, 2 and 3  b) 1 and 2 only  c) 2 and 3 only  d) 1 and 3 only
**Correct Answer:** a) 1, 2 and 3

The **business of parliamentary committees is transacted in Hindi or English (statement 2)**, and their **minutes/records are prepared in Hindi or English (statement 3)**. All three are correct (a). *(A polity item.)*

**Q.46 (CDS 2022-I)** — 1. The Committee on Estimates is an ad hoc committee of Parliament. 2. The Committee on Railway Convention is a standing committee of Parliament.

**Correct Answer:** d) Neither 1 nor 2
**Explanation:** Parliamentary committees are of two types — **standing** (permanent, constituted regularly) and **ad hoc** (temporary, for a specific purpose). **Q.103 (CDS 2022-I)** — What is "Unicorn Company" often mentioned in Indian news?

**Correct Answer:** a) Any privately held startup company with a value of over 1 billion

**Q.29 (CDS 2022-II)** — In India, which body is responsible for implementing the Government's policy on all matters concerning telecommunications?

**Q.40 (CDS 2022-II)** — Which statement about the Public Accounts Committee (PAC) is not correct?

**Q.92 (CDS 2023-I)** — Which statement about the Solicitor General of India (SGI) is not correct?

**Q.93 (CDS 2023-I)** — Which committee does not have members from the Rajya Sabha?

**Correct Answer:** c) The Estimates Committee

**Q.6 (CDS 2024-I)** — Which was not a part of the action programme of the Non-Cooperation Movement?
a) The Congress organisation was to reach down to the village/mohalla level.  b) Boycott of government-affiliated schools and colleges.  c) Taking control of the State's law-and-order machinery by the Congress Working Committee.  d) Surrender of titles and honours given by the government.
**Correct Answer:** c)
**Explanation:** The **Non-Cooperation Movement (1920-22)** was, by definition, about **withdrawing cooperation** from the colonial state through peaceful means — **surrendering titles and honours (d)**, **boycotting government schools, colleges, courts and foreign cloth (b)**, and **strengthening the Congress organisation down to the village/mohalla level (a)**. It did **not** aim to **seize the State's law-and-order machinery (c)** — that would be a violent insurrection, contrary to its non-violent, non-cooperationist character. Hence c is the odd one out. *(A history item.)*

**Q.74 (CDS 2024-I)** — Which are included in the M1 definition of money? 1. Reserves 2. Currency 3. Time deposits 4. Demand deposits.
a) 1 and 3 only  b) 2 and 3  c) 2 and 4  d) 1, 3 and 4
**Correct Answer:** c) 2 and 4
**Explanation:** **M1 (Narrow Money)** is the most liquid measure of money — funds available for immediate transactions. It comprises **currency with the public (2)**, **demand deposits (4)** (and "other deposits" with the RBI). **Time deposits (3)** are *not* part of M1 — they are locked in for a period and are added only in the broader **M3**. "Reserves" (1) (banks' balances with the RBI) belong to *reserve money*, not M1. Hence M1 = currency + demand deposits = 2 and 4 (c).

**Q.91 (CDS 2024-II)** — 1. A rupee Vostro account is one that an Indian bank holds for a foreign bank in rupees, enabling domestic banks to provide international banking services. 2. Insurance density (average premium per capita) does not fully capture the financing gap on premature death because most Indian insurance products are savings-linked with a small protection component.
a) 1 only  b) 2 only  c) Both 1 and 2  d) Neither 1 nor 2
**Correct Answer:** c) Both 1 and 2
**Explanation:** A **Vostro account** ("your account with us") is an account a domestic (Indian) bank maintains **on behalf of a foreign bank, in the domestic currency (rupees)** — Special Rupee Vostro Accounts have been used to enable **rupee-based international trade settlement** (statement 1, correct). On insurance, **"insurance density"** (premium per capita) overstates protection because **most Indian life-insurance products are savings/investment-linked with only a thin pure-protection element**, so the true **protection (mortality) gap is larger** than density suggests (statement 2, correct). Both are correct (c).
